\section{Parametric Tests}\label{parametric-tests}

\subsection{1. Two-Sample T-test:Life Expectancy by Economic
Status}\label{two-sample-t-testlife-expectancy-by-economic-status}

\textbf{Research Question:} Is there a significant difference in the
mean life expectancy between developed and developing nations?\\

\textbf{Hypothesis}\\
* \textbf{Null Hypothesis}
\(H_0: \mu_{\text{developed}} = \mu_{\text{developing}}\) (There is no
difference in mean life expectancy).\\
* \textbf{Alternate Hypothesis}
\(H_1: \mu_{\text{developed}} \neq \mu_{\text{developing}}\) (There is a
significant difference).\\

Our assumptions for this test were as follows:\\
* \textbf{Independence}: The observations are independent countries.\\

* \textbf{Variance}: In order to account for the potential unequal
variance of the two groups,we shall use the Welch's Two-Sample t-test
which does not assume equal variance.\\

\subsection{2. Analysis of Variance
(ANOVA)}\label{analysis-of-variance-anova}

\textbf{Research Question:} Is there a significant difference in the
mean life expectancy across different geographical regions in the
world?\\

\textbf{Hypothesis}\\
* \textbf{Null Hypothesis} \(H_0: \mu_1 = \mu_2 = ...= \mu_k\) (There is
no difference in mean life expectancy across different geographical
regions).\\
* \textbf{Alternate Hypothesis}
\(H_1: \mu_1 \neq \mu_2 \neq ... \neq \mu_k\) (There is a difference in
mean life expectancy across different geographical regions or at least
one geographical mean is different from the rest).\\

Our assumptions for this test were as follows:\\
* \textbf{Independence}: The observations are independent countries.\\
* \textbf{Normality}: Assumption of the residuals (difference between
observed values and group means) are normally distributed as well as
being guided by the Central Limit Theorem, it allowed us to continue
with the Parametric test due to the large sample size (\(N = 2864\))\\
* \textbf{Homogeneity of Variance}: ANOVA assumes that variance of each
group is roughly equal (Homoskedasticity).\\

\subsection{3. Multiple Linear
Regression}\label{multiple-linear-regression}

indicators, healthcare of a country, physical metrics and demographic
scales such as region predict the life expectancy of a country? Which of
these variables are the most critical in determining the life expectancy
of a country?\\

\textbf{Hypothesis}\\
* \textbf{Null Hypothesis}
\(H_0: \beta_1 = \beta_2 = \dots = \beta_k = 0\) (None of the predictor
variables have a significant linear relationship with life
expectancy).\\
* \textbf{Alternate Hypothesis} \(H_1: \beta_i \neq 0\) (At least one
predictor is statistically significant).\\

Our assumptions for this test were as follows:\\
* \textbf{Linearity}: There is a linear relationship between dependent
and independent variables.\\
* \textbf{Independence of errors}: Assumption of no autocorrelation
(thus year will be omitted in the regression model).\\
* \textbf{Homogeneity of Variance}: Residuals must have a constant
variance (Homoskedasticity).\\
* \textbf{Normality of Residuals}: Residuals must be normally
distributed.\\
* \textbf{No multicollinearity}: The predictors are independent of each
other.\\

\section{Non-Parametric Tests}\label{non-parametric-tests}

Non-parametric tests would neutralize severe skewness and ignore unequal
variances thus providing robust results. This is by focusing on the
median ranks and not the raw means.\\

\subsection{1. Mann-Whitney U Test (Wilcoxon Rank-Sum
Test)}\label{mann-whitney-u-test-wilcoxon-rank-sum-test}

\emph{This is the non-parametric equivalent of the Welch T-Test}\\

\textbf{Research Question:} Is there a significant difference in the
distribution of life expectancy between developed and developing
nations?\\

\textbf{Hypothesis}\\
* \textbf{Null Hypothesis} \(H_0:\) The distribution of life expectancy
is the same for both developed and developing countries.\\
* \textbf{Alternate Hypothesis} \(H_1:\) The distribution of life
expectancy differs significantly between developed and developing
nations.\\

The Mann-Whitney U Test neutralises the exhibited skew.\\

\begin{Shaded}
\begin{Highlighting}[]
\NormalTok{mann\_whitney }\OtherTok{\textless{}{-}} \FunctionTok{wilcox.test}\NormalTok{(Life\_expectancy }\SpecialCharTok{\textasciitilde{}}\NormalTok{ Economy\_status\_Developed, }\AttributeTok{data =}\NormalTok{ df\_clean)}
\FunctionTok{print}\NormalTok{(mann\_whitney)}
\end{Highlighting}
\end{Shaded}

\begin{verbatim}
## 
##  Wilcoxon rank sum test with continuity correction
## 
## data:  Life_expectancy by Economy_status_Developed
## W = 85918, p-value < 2.2e-16
## alternative hypothesis: true location shift is not equal to 0
\end{verbatim}

The Wilcoxon rank sum test gave us a p-value which was much smaller than
our alpha of 0.05. We reject the null hypothesis and conclude that there
the distribution of life expectancy differs significantly between
developed and developing nations. The developed nations indeed exhibit a
higher median life expectancy than developing ones.\\

\subsection{2. Kruskal-Wallis Test}\label{kruskal-wallis-test}

\emph{This is the non-parametric equivalent of One Way ANOVA}\\

\textbf{Research Question:} Is there a significant difference in
distribution of life expectancy across different geographical regions in
the world?\\

\textbf{Hypothesis}\\
* \textbf{Null Hypothesis} \(H_0:\) The distribution of life expectancy
is the same for all geographical regions.\\
* \textbf{Alternate Hypothesis} \(H_1:\) At least one geographical
region's distribution differs from others.\\

Kruskal Wallis test does not assume homoskedasticity or normality thus
an appropriate alternative to the previous ANOVA test.\\

## data:  Life_expectancy by Region

\end{verbatim}

Kruskal-Wallis test gave us a p-value which was close to zero meaning it
was highly significant. We reject the null hypothesis and conclude that
different geographical regions possess different levels of life
expectancy. The distribution of life expectancy is not the same
worldwide.\\

\subsection{3. Spearman Rank
Correlation}\label{spearman-rank-correlation}

\emph{This evaluates the non-linear relationship prevalent in the
Multiple Linear Regression}\\

\textbf{Research Question:} Is there a significant relationship between
years of schooling and life expectancy?\\

\textbf{Hypothesis}\\
* \textbf{Null Hypothesis} \(H_0:\) There is no monotonic relationship
between schooling and life expectancy (\(\rho_s = 0\)).\\
* \textbf{Alternate Hypothesis} \(H_1:\) There is a monotonic
relationship between schooling and life expectancy
(\(\rho_s \neq 0\)).\\

Due to Spearman Rank focussing on Ranks, it evaluates the relationships
based on how the values behave; monotonic ranks. If one variable goes
up, the other goes up.\\

\end{verbatim}

The Spearman Rank Test gave us a p-value which was less than 0.05 and
much closer to zero indicating significance. Due to having a very low
p-value, we reject the null hypothesis and conclude that there is a
monotonic relationship between schooling and life expectancy. We
obtained a Spearman coefficient of \(\approx 0.74\) which indicated a
strong monotonic relationship. This informed us that, as schooling years
increased, life expectancy increased. This cemented our earlier argument
in including \texttt{Schooling} despite having the baseline VIF score of
\(\approx 5\).\\
